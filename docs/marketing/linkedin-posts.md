### Post 2: Tactical Advice
**Hook** (first 2 lines):
Your 1-on-1s are boring because you're asking the wrong questions.

Stop asking "How's everything going?"

**Body**:
Your 1-on-1s are boring because you're asking the wrong questions.

Stop asking "How's everything going?"

Here are 5 questions that actually surface real problems:

1. "What's the most frustrating part of your week right now?"
2. "If you could change one thing about how our team works, what would it be?"
3. "What's something you've wanted to tell me but haven't?"
4. "Where do you feel like you're wasting time?"
5. "What would make next week better than this one?"

The pattern: specificity forces honesty.

"How's it going" gets "fine."

"What's frustrating you" gets real answers.

One more tip — after they answer, shut up. Count to 5 in your head. The silence is where the real stuff comes out.

Try one of these in your next 1-on-1 and tell me what happens.

### Post 5: Tactical Advice
**Hook** (first 2 lines):
The feedback framework that actually works in 30 seconds.

No sandwiches. No scripts. Just this:

**Body**:
The feedback framework that actually works in 30 seconds.

No sandwiches. No scripts. Just this:

"When you [specific behavior], it [specific impact]. I'd like to see [specific change]."

That's it. Three sentences.

Example: "When you go silent in standups and then bring up blockers on Friday, it means the team can't help you during the week. I'd like you to flag blockers the day they come up, even if you're not sure yet."

Why this works:

- Behavior, not character. You're not saying "you're bad at communicating." You're pointing at a specific thing they did.
- Impact, not opinion. "It frustrates me" is about you. "The team can't help" is about the outcome.
- Request, not demand. "I'd like to see" leaves room for a conversation.

The biggest feedback mistake managers make: waiting until it's a big deal. Give feedback when it's small and the stakes are low.

Nobody gets defensive about a 30-second conversation on Tuesday. They absolutely will about a 45-minute "we need to talk" on Friday.

What's your go-to feedback approach?

### Post 8: Tactical Advice
**Hook** (first 2 lines):
3 phrases that immediately de-escalate conflict on your team.

Bookmark this. You'll need it.

**Body**:
3 phrases that immediately de-escalate conflict on your team.

Bookmark this. You'll need it.

1. "Help me understand your perspective."

Not "explain yourself." Not "why did you do that." This phrase signals genuine curiosity. It takes the other person out of defense mode.

2. "I think we want the same outcome. Let's figure out where we're disagreeing on the path."

This reframes the conflict. You're not opponents. You're two people with different maps trying to reach the same destination.

3. "I might be wrong about this, but here's what I'm seeing."

Vulnerability disarms. When you admit you could be wrong, the other person doesn't have to fight to be right.

These aren't tricks. They work because they're genuinely respectful.

The goal of conflict isn't to win. It's to find the best answer. These phrases get you there faster.

These aren't things you pick up by reading a blog post during your first week as a manager.

### Post 13: Tactical Advice
**Hook** (first 2 lines):
How to say "no" to your CEO without torpedoing your career.

A 4-step script that works every time:

**Body**:
How to say "no" to your CEO without torpedoing your career.

A 4-step script that works every time:

Step 1: Validate the goal, not the request.
"I understand we need to hit X by Q2. That makes sense."

Step 2: Show what you see that they don't.
"Here's what I'm concerned about — if we add this now, it delays Y by 3 weeks."

Step 3: Offer an alternative.
"What if we scoped it down to just the core feature and shipped in 1 week instead?"

Step 4: Let them decide.
"I'm happy to go either direction. Just want to make sure you have the full picture."

Why this works:

You're not saying "no." You're saying "yes, and here's the tradeoff."

Most people who struggle to push back are afraid of being seen as negative or uncommitted. This approach makes you look MORE committed because you're thinking about the outcome, not just the task.

The founders and leaders I respect most are the ones who want their team to push back. That's how better decisions get made.

What's a time you successfully pushed back on a bad idea?

**Hook** (first 2 lines):
The most productive team I've ever been on had a weird rule.

No feedback in Slack. Ever.

**Body**:
The most productive team I've ever been on had a weird rule.

No feedback in Slack. Ever.

If you had feedback for someone, you had to say it live. Call, Zoom, in person. No exceptions.

At first I thought it was overkill. Why can't I just send a quick message?

Then I realized what was actually happening.

In Slack, feedback gets watered down. You over-explain. You add qualifiers. You use "maybe" and "just a thought" to soften it. The person reads it in whatever mood they're in and fills in the tone themselves.

On a call, you hear tone. You can adjust in real time. You can say "that came out harsher than I meant" and course-correct in seconds.

The team wasn't avoiding feedback. They were protecting it. Making sure it actually landed the way it was intended.

After a few weeks, something interesting happened: people gave MORE feedback, not less. Because they trusted the format.

A small rule. A huge impact on trust.
